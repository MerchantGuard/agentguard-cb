## Card network rules

Visa Core Rules and Visa Product and Service Rules, as well as Mastercard Chargeback Guide rules, govern the form and content of evidence submitted in chargeback dispute representments. Submitting falsified, fabricated, or knowingly inaccurate evidence may violate those rules and may result in fines, holds, reserve requirements, or termination of the merchant's processing relationship by the merchant's acquirer.

## Stripe Services Agreement

Section 8 of the Stripe Services Agreement governs how merchants respond to disputes. The merchant remains responsible for compliance with the SSA and Stripe's published Dispute documentation. See <https://stripe.com/legal/ssa>.

## State unfair and deceptive trade practices statutes (UDAP)

Submission of knowingly false or misleading evidence in a chargeback dispute may, in some jurisdictions, give rise to liability under state UDAP statutes. Examples include:

- California Business and Professions Code § 17200 (Unfair Competition Law)
- New York General Business Law § 349 (Deceptive Acts and Practices)
- Florida Statutes § 501.204 (Florida Deceptive and Unfair Trade Practices Act)

This list is illustrative and not exhaustive. The applicability of any particular statute depends on the facts of each case. Consult counsel.

## Federal wire fraud — 18 U.S.C. § 1343

Knowingly false or fabricated evidence transmitted by interstate wires (including via Stripe's API) may, where the elements of the statute are met, give rise to exposure under 18 U.S.C. § 1343. We do not assert that every wrongful submission is automatically wire fraud. Whether the elements are met depends on the specific facts. Consult counsel before submitting any evidence about which the merchant is uncertain.

## Personal information — GDPR / CCPA / similar

The software processes personally identifiable information (PII) such as customer email addresses, IP addresses, device identifiers, and shipping addresses. The merchant is responsible for documenting a lawful basis for processing this data under applicable privacy law. In the EU, Article 6(1)(f) of the GDPR (legitimate interests) is the most commonly cited basis for processing customer PII for the purpose of defending against payment disputes; the merchant must conduct its own legitimate-interests balancing analysis and document the result.
